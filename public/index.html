About us

Who we are?

Establishment

EPaRDA was established by professionals representing different backgrounds who have extensive experience in working with the pastoral communities and and re-registered as Ethiopian residents Charity in 2010 with Charities and Societies Agency of Federal Democratic Republic of Ethiopia.

Vision and Mission

Our vision is to see Ethiopian pastoralists become self-reliant and their livelihoods improved. And it is our mission to conduct action research on overall issues of pastoral communities and undertaking integrated development activities in pastoral areas.

Operational Area

The organization’s area of operation so far has been the south omo zone. In addition, the organization is making the necessary preparation and assessment to expand its interventions to Bench- Magi and Borena zones.

KEY PROGRAM ACHIEVEMENTS:

Food security and improving livelihood: five small-scale irrigation sites were developed; in the livestock sector - improving animal husbandry, establishing veterinary clinics, training of animal health workers, construction of livestock markets and introducing camels were conducted; modern fishery and beekeeping practices have been introduced as income generating enterprises; and pastoralist cooperatives unions were also formed to improve and utilize local business opportunities.

Water development: EPaRDA has so far constructed four ponds and one subsurface dam.

Education: six alternative basic education centers were established, and a sum of 300 students are enrolled. These centers later grow to formal schools.

Health: mobile clinics accessible to 80,000 people have been in operation with preventive and clinical services, ten birthing huts were constructed and equipped; nine health centers and over 30 health posts were equipped, health professionals have got different professional trainings, community volunteers were trained as community health promoters, monthly outreach immunization for children under five and women in child bearing age is conducted in more than 80 kebeles, and also a pioneering interventions of health insurance scheme and drug revolving funds were introduced.

HIV/AIDS: awareness creation, VCT training, training of volunteers and HIV positive people, organizing forums for discussions, launching of mobile outreach VCT services, and establishing and strengthening associations of people living with HIV/AIDS, youth clubs and school clubs.

Gender: EPaRDA has been trying to address gender equity and women’s empowerment through awareness creation workshops, training and provision of alternative livelihoods (like small business).

Emergencies: EPaRDA had been engaged in emergency operations during severe flooding Omo river in 2006 and 2007, and also in 2010 the Acute Watery Diarrhea emergency. The operations were focused on relief, human health, and livestock health.

Peace building: EPaRDA has been a pioneer in attempting to facilitate and organize in-country as well as cross border (Ethiopia and Kenya) conflict mitigation and peace building initiatives. It has conducted a series of peace making processes among the different ethnic groups in South Omo, the Borana and Wata from Borana zone and furthermore the Turkana and Gebra from Kenyan side took part.

Capacity building: The capacity strengthening efforts of EPaRDA include three major components, namely, community based capacity building program (include infrastructure development such as ponds/wells, grain-mills, non-formal schools and market places), capacity building for government offices (include training, material support), and enhancing EPaRDA’s field office capabilities for effective delivery of community services.

Research: EPaRDA believes that research-based development interventions will have a long lasting sustainable impact across generations and societies. As a result, EPaRDA has conducted several pieces of research on a diverse range of issues, including agronomy, socio-economy, gender, and peace building.

Conducting Researches

Eparda is engaged in conducting action researches and studies in selected areas of development and peace.

The South Omo pastoralist communities are marginalized and live in a remote area. As a result, much is not known about the communities and their surroundings. Providing fruitful development interventions in the area requires sufficient knowledge about the situations on the ground. It is in this context that EPaRDA, as implied in its name, is engaged in action-oriented researches and studies in selected areas of development and peace. Since its establishment, EPaRDA conducted several researches and studies have been conducted and practical steps have been taken based on the findings. The following are the major studies conducted to date.

Ethno veterinary research

A study on indigenous herbal practices of South Omo pastoralists against external parasite was conducted in Tsemay wereda. The objectives of the study include: to ascertain the efficiency of plant species previously used by pastoralists of South Omo, to treat animals infested with helminthes parasites, to identify new plant species to be used in the treatment of livestock and to teach the pastoral communities about the protection and conservation of these medicinal plants in the coming 10 years through the use of extension workers.

Resource inventory and mapping

Resource inventory and mapping survey was conducted. As a result of the survey key rangeland and water areas, shared resources for the livestock and wildlife, dry land reserves and potential conflict areas, migratory pattern of the target pastoral communities and land use pattern of agro pastoral communities were identified and recommendations were drawn. It is being used for various interventions in the area.

Socio-economic survey

The socio- economic survey on the economic and nutritional return of pastoralisim, agro-pastoralisim and rain fed/irrigated farming was conducted.

Camel adaptability trial

Considering that the area does not have much grazing land rather better suited for browsers, EPaRDA took the initiative to introduce camels into the project area as action research. Camel adaptability trial was done for a year with 10 camels brought in the project area. The result was successful and EPaRDA is providing the communities with camels and other organizations are adopting the method.

Dry meat research

This was one of the trials conducted with the aim of facilitating livestock marketing. For the purpose, demonstration slaughterhouse and meat drying house were constructed. The trial was carried out for over three months. The preliminary results showed that, it is profitable to sell livestock in the form of dry meat rather than live animals that incur cost from additional transportation and other related costs.

Sorghum transplantation

Since the area is suffering from shortage of rain, the rain that falls is not enough to grow crops till seed setting. The crops usually dry without setting seed. In order to solve this problem, EPaRDA conducted sorghum transplantation trial. The outcome of the trial showed that the seedlings transplanted performed well and set seeds earlier than the crops planted. EPaRDA shared the outcomes with the communities and implementation is underway.

Harmonization of modern and traditional laws and gender in peace building – the case of South Omo

This study investigates ways of harmonizing traditional and modern laws for a more fruitful peace building process in the area. In addition, the study tried to take into consideration the role of women in peace building. The outcome has been used for various peace building interventions in the area.

Post conflict trauma Rehabilitation study

Due to recurrent inter ethnic conflict and the post conflict trauma that usually last long in the communities, EPaRDA conducted post conflict trauma rehabilitation study. The study tried to identify ways and methodologies to overcome the trauma.

Agronomy study in the four woredas of the South Omo Zone

This study focuses mainly on analyzing the existing farming practices in Bena Tsemay, Hamer, Dassench and Nyangatom woredas. The study is conducted by taking into account traditional farming practices of the communities. It demonstrates the great contribution of dry land farming to enhance food security in the study areas by introducing improved technologies.

Best practices documentation

The documentation of best practices and important project activities has been in place in EPaRDA with a view of sharing experiences and consolidate efforts. EPaRDA prepared various audiovisual materials and publications of the best practices in various sectors of intervention to use them as a source document in the future endeavor and for other interested stakeholders.

Livelihood Strengthening

Livestock, Apiculture Promotion, Fishery, Small Scale Irrigation and Water Development

Livestock

The most common character of pastoralists is their acquaintance with livestock in their livelihood. In South Omo Zone, by and large, the communities’ livelihood depends mainly on livestock. Ensuring food security in the area should primarily focus on making use of the existing large livestock resource. However, a poor animal health situation and backward system production is the main features that affect the livestock productivity. Eventually, the communities’ livelihood is subject to chronic food insecurity.

Making use of what the surrounding provides is always a strategy for all food security and other interventions of EPaRDA. In this regard, EPaRDA has designed projects that ensure utmost utili zation of the communities’ livestock resource by improving the poor health situations and production systems. In this regard several projects that directly or indirectly related to livestock resource of the communities of South Omo are designed and implemented in cooperation with partner organizations and stockholders.

A base line survey on animal health and productivity is conducted in order to guide EPaRDA and other stockholders interventions. EPaRDA’s livestock health interventions in South Omo Zone have above all improved the livestock health situation. Under this program, the trainings of Community Animal Health Workers from within the communities are conducted and have contributed a lot in creating improved access to livestock health services in the area. Likewise, the repeatedly conducted livestock vaccinations have widely reduced the prevalence of livestock disease epidemics.

Though Camel is a remarkably performing drought resistant livestock in other pastoral areas of Ethiopia, it was not know by the communities of South Omo. Taking note of that EPaRDA implemented Camel adaptability trial in South Omo Zone and achieve successful results. This is one of the important initiatives, which EPaRDA embarked up on for food security reasons. According to the information gathered from Asile, Kola-kaja and Shala-luka kebeles, where EPaRDA has hitherto distributed camels, their number has grown up remarkably and they are reproducing very well. As a result of this, currently the beneficiary communities are effectively utilizing the camels as a source of milk, meat and transport. After observing the wide benefits of camels, there has grown a wide interest among the communities in South Omo and a lot of pastoralists have currently registered to own camels even on full cost recovery basis. Such interventions are vital in boosting the pastoralist food security

Agriculture Promotion

In South Omo Zone, mainly in areas with vegetation and flora, and supportive weather condition, beekeeping is has a good potential. The communities practice traditional beekeeping. However, the traditional beekeeping practice was less productive. Therefore, promotion of modern apiculture is one of the food security supplementary initiatives of EPaRDA in the area.

Accordingly, twenty interested traditional beekeepers were identified as a model. They were trained in beekeeping/apicultural production techniques, beehives management and honey handling methods, and beehives were distributed to them. The beneficiaries are presently enjoying improved honey production as a result of the support. They are getting additional incomes for their household that ultimately contributes to their food security. Following the success of the first round model beneficiaries, other community groups as well as concerned government offices are requesting for similar supports. Currently, EPaRDA is responding to these demands and the initiative will be strengthened.

Fishery

Therefore, fishery promotion is another vital initiative launched by EPaRDA for improving the food security of the pastoralist communities.

The communities of Dassenech Woreda, that lives along Lake Turkana practice traditional fishing in small amount. Similar to other pastoralist communities in the area, the Dassenech community mainly engaged in livestock raring. They were living with chronic problems related to food security. It is in a view of creating alternative means of livelihood that EPaRDA designed a project that give the community an opportunity to make use of the resource that exist in their surrounding, the Omo River. The project was funded by Cordaid.

Accordingly, 16 fishing boats and 65 fishing nets provided to about 154 households. They are using the fish products both for home consumption and for marketing to obtain additional income. EPaRDA has also linked the beneficiaries with the Ethiopian Fish Corporation for the purpose of creating sustainable market opportunity to the pastoralist fishermen. In addition to this, EPaRDA, in collaboration with the Ethiopian Fish Corporation, trained those fishing groups on fishing techniques, boats operation, and use of the high yielding fishing nets. Moreover, around 15 women were trained in fishing - net making and on how to market their produce. The fishermen are linked with the net-making women so that they can get additional nets when the need arises

Small Scale Irrigation

Primarily, utilizing what is available in the surrounding is the focus of EPaRDA’s interventions. What advantages does the environment provides in meeting the food security problem? In the case of South Omo, the two permanent rivers, the Omo and the Waito, should come first. The communities that live alongside the rivers usually practice flood retreat farming. They farm after the rivers over flow and water the surrounding of the riverbank. It is in this consideration that EPaRDA started to introduce modern Small Scale Irrigation and strengthening the traditional ones.

EPaRDA has so far introduced food security enhancing irrigation cultivation in five sites namely Gola and Karo (Hamer woreda); Lochuch and Selegn (Dassenech woreda); and Omo Kibish (Nyangatom woreda). In these irrigation schemes, around 2000 pastoralist and agro-pastoralist households have benefited and harvested about 10,000 quintals of food grains (maize and sorghum mainly) from a total area close to 500 hectares. As a result of this magnificent impact, the communities in these locations have relatively became food secure as compared to other community groups in South Omo, and this situation can be understood from the assertion made by the Gola communities in Hamer, “We do not need food aid, rather we are in the position of donating food to our relatives”

Water Development

Water scarcity, erratic rainfall, scarcity of hydrological and other related information, limited promotion of water harvesting, conflict over water resources and the spread of water born diseases have long been known as the prominent challenges facing the pastoralist communities in South Omo Zone. As a result of this, EPaRDA pursued the development of water infrastructures, especially water ponds, and the strengthening of community water management capacities in the target areas. EPaRDA has so far undertaken construction of four water ponds in Gisma (Bena-Tsemay woreda), Arbore (Hamer woreda), Omorate zuria (Dassenech woreda) and Nyangatom woreda, and established water use committees and provide training on water utilization.

Capacity Building

Tranings on wide range of issues and material support.

EPaRDA provides capacity building initiatives for pastoral communities in three areas, namely: community based capacity building programs; capacity building of various government experts through material support and various trainings on wide range of issues on patoralists life and enhancing EPaRDA’s field office capabilities for effective delivery of community services.

The strengthening of community capacity through infrastructure development such as ponds/wells, surface dams, grain mills, non-formal schools, birthing hats, market places, latrines and others were conducted. EPaRDA also provided irrigation pumps, fishing gear; beehives and other apiculture equipment; farm implements; improved seeds; motor bikes for government offices, especially to health centers; and medicines for humans and livestock as well as medical equipment to woreda and zonal health centers.

EPaRDA provides training to community groups in different areas including hygiene and sanitation, water conservation and management, latrine construction and management, malaria prevention and control, HIV/AIDS prevention, fishery and fishing net production, bee keeping and bee hives management and honey handling, lowland agriculture production and irrigation management, crop husbandry livestock health, camel and goat husbandry practices and management, micro-enterprise establishment and management, book keeping and accounting, project designing and reparation, institutional management , monitoring and evaluation system, and conflict management.

Human Health and HIV/AIDS

Provide basic integrated services, particularly focusing on needs of women and children.

The major threats to pastoralists’ health stems from: frequent drought and flooding, malaria, and women’s and children’s medical problems resulting from child birth. Prevalence of traditional harmful practices such as the giving of birth alone outside of home remains a big challenge. EPaRDA’s main objective in this sector is to provide basic integrated services, particularly focusing on needs of women and children.
The main interventions so far EPaRDA has undertaken include:
• Establishment of mobile clinics accessible to 80,000 people
• Constructed and equipped of eleven birth huts with trained attendants,
• Training of community health workers to carry out immunization ,
• Introduce health insurance scheams and establishing over 20 Health Insurance groups ,
• Offering of basic health education through youth clubs ,
• Establishing pastoralist health committees,
• Equip health centers with safe abortion kit, safe delivery kit and contraceptives,
• Health education through mobile outreach camps,
• Training of tradition Birth Attendants first to enhance their skill and provide with delivery kits; and later shifting their role as only health promoters that works in strengthening referral linkage to health facilities and promote institutional delivery.
• support monthly outreach Extended Program of Immunization for almost a decade that covers over 60 kebeles ,
• Establishing Revolving Drug Fund,
• prepare and distribute traditionally appropriate Health Information and Education materials ,
• provide various trainings for Health professionals on Basic Emergency Management of Obstetric Care, Safe and Clean Delivery, STI diagnosis and management protocol, family planning, and other health subjects,
• Support mobile VCT activates with logistical support and
• Research, Knowledge Transfer & Networking

Approaching HIV/AIDS interventions among communities require a thorough understanding of the cultural nuances of the communities in question. EPaRDA, quickly recognized that as long as early interventions are not done in prevention and awareness creation, the HIV/AIDS pandemic could pose a serious challenge to development work in pastoral areas of South Omo zone. A survey conducted by EPaRDA in 2006 revealed that the level of awareness of the pastoral communities towards HIV/AIDs in South Omo zone was at only 11.6%. In addition, only 10.6% of the respondents identified three or more ways of being infected with HIV/AIDS and means of prevention. The information gap on the HIV/AIDS epidemic is influenced by factors such as the area being one of the most marginalized areas of the country, the communities’ transhumant lifestyle and traditional practices that encourage having multiple sexual partners. There is also scarcity of partners for testing, education and treatment of diseases.

In the face of these challenges, EPaRDA launched and implemented a project in 2002 in the woredas where there had been no prior intervention and since then EPaRDA has been intervening on HIV/AIDS as crosscutting issue. A subsequent project began in 2008, focused upon bringing about the necessary behavioral changes by improving the awareness and capacity of the community towards the transmission and prevention of the disease.

The approaches taken by EPaRDA include: Awareness creation; Voluntary Counseling and Testing (VCT) training; training of traditional healers in preventive HIV/AIDS counseling; training of volunteers and HIV positive people on HIV/AIDS education and advocacy; organizing forums for discussions, such as workshops, youth and music clubs; the establishment of sexual and reproductive health clubs; launching of mobile outreach VCT services; the production and distribution of educational materials, as well as the establishment of associations of people living with HIV/AIDS. It is equally important that a more aggressive approach to improving the provision of antiretroviral therapy (ART) is taken.

Education

Non-Formal Education

Non-formal education has played a critical role in the capacity building program of EPaRDA. This was a project initiated by the community after assessing EPaRDA’s successful performance in other capacity building initiatives in areas such as livestock keeping, income generating and others mentioned earlier. In 2002, the pastoralist community requested EPaRDA for introduction of a non-formal education system relevant to their life style. EPaRDA in collaboration with PACT Ethiopia established six non-formal education sites Birale, Bilibilo, kola Keja, karo, Shaba Mero and Shalla in Bena -Tsemay and Hamer woredas. The project targeted 300 children for non - formal education and facilitated the project by provision of materials, training centers, trained manpower and formation of school management committee in collaboration with the communities. Among the targeted 300 students 90% registered at the initial stage and later on 172 more adults joined the program and a total of 443 benefited.

Emergency Support

Emergency relief, Disaster preparedness and Risk aversion operation.

The patoralists in the South Omo zone where EPaRDA has been working for are vulnerable for natural hazardous such as drought and flood. EPaRDA’s pioneering effort was put in place in 2005 with funding from cord aid. A contingency fund system was developed in 2005 within this project and a start up project of 250,000 Ethiopian birr was allocated. This has helped in carrying out swift emergency operations to save the lives of victims of flood and was a remarkable step in overcoming the delays in emergency supplies that result from the usual lengthy process of fund appropriation at the time of emergencies.

On August 13, 2006, the Omo River flooded around 14 villages in the Dassenech and Nyangatom woredas of the zone. Consequently, 364 people were killed, 21,523 people were displaced, and more than 3,200 livestock died and around 1,225 local grain storages containing 2,280 quintals of grain were destroyed.

EPaRDA was the first to appear on the scene of the incident with emergency support a day after the flood. EPaRDA’s operations were focused on relief, human health, livestock health, and emergency peace and security administration interventions. The interventions were through the funds provided by cord aid, Health Unlimited, Pastoralist Forum Ethiopia (PFE), Oxfam GB and the United Nations Office of Humanitarian Affairs (UNOCHA). The EPaRDA health team composed of 2 medical doctors and two nurses gave humanitarian assistance and the necessary support to 22,544 flood victims. The majority were women, children and elders. There was no early preparedness for such a crisis on the government side, so EPaRDA mobilized emergency funds from its health project funded by Health unlimited to cover the cost of medicines, daily subsistence allowance, and logistics support for government heath staff. Furthermore, EPaRDA secured emergency funds form Oxfam GB, cord aid and PFE for hygiene and environment sanitation to purchase and distribute materials for displaced people during the first phase of the emergency operations. Disease like pneumonia, dysentery, measles and others emerged as a result of this crisis. EPaRDa deployed a medical team led by a doctor to provide emergency care to the displaced people. Also vaccinations and treatment were given to 428,133 livestock against different diseases that would have caused an outbreak following the flooding.

Similarly, the flooding that occurred in 2007 in three woredas: Hammer, Dassenech and Nyangatom, was devastating both in its extent and geographic coverage compared to the 2006 flood. Early preparations made by EPaRDA in collaboration with donors and government-especially at woreda and zonal level saved lives and properties.

The threat of flooding of the Omo and Woito rivers is a regular phenomenon hitting the low-lying woredas of south-Omo zone: Dassenech, Nyaangatom, and Hamer. From the past experiences, the flooding of the Omo River starts from Mid-August and continues until the first week of October.

In Addition to conducting emergency relief efforts, EPaRDA is engaged in disaster preparedness and risk aversion operations. The risk aversion activities were put into practice in 2009 and helped to save the communities from the possible destruction of the flood. EPaRDA has at disposal emergency contingency projects to support the communities for any unforeseen circumstances.

Acute Watery Diarrhea (AWD) prevention was also conducted in Dassenech woreda in partnership with Pact Ethiopia funded by UN OCHA in 2010.

Women Empowerment and IGA

Awareness Creation Workshops

Women perform a diverse and wide workload in pastoralist communities including: constructing shelters, fetching water, collecting firewood, preparing food, taking care of children and the livestock and working on their small seasonal farms. However, their contribution is not recognized and there is significant imbalance between what women do and what women have. EPaRDA has been striving to empower women in its intervention area through awareness creation workshops, training and provision of alternative livelihoods, and the introduction of labour saving mechanisms such as grinding mills. A workshop on gender awareness for subject matter specialist and the community was conducted in 2001 in Hamer and Bena-Tsemay woredas for 44 participants. EPaRDA has also introduced the involvement of women on conflict resolution which is later recognized the prominent role of women in decision-making. Women alongside the youth and elder representatives from Borana, Arbore and Hamer ethnic groups actively participated in the peace conference and traditional peace ceremonies held at Elkuni TeleTele woreda of Borana and then at Arbore and Asile of Hamer Woredas respectively, During the event the women had opportunity to air their views on various issues concerning peace and security in their localities.

Other interventions included the establishments of women’s petty trading enterprises, women’s goat breeding groups, and women’s credit and saving groups. A 26 member women group, both goat beneficiaries and others, organized themselves into credit and saving groups. They contributed 3 birr each and with their own total initial capital 78 birr and project money birr 1,000 added and they started petty trade. A 16-strong Braille Group was also formed into credit and saving association. Their initial capital was birr 48 and that was supplemented by birr 500 loan from the project. These income generating groups have done well and hence encouraged more people to join the scheme. 120 women from 4 kebeles of Dassencech woreda have been organized into 6 credit and saving groups since 2014/2015.

Peace Building

Conflict Mitigation and Peace Building

In pastoralist areas the major source of conflicts revolve around access to the use of natural resources, mainly water and pasture. There are also other sources of conflict emanating from the cultural practices and heroism, and asset building through cattle raiding. In addition, the demographic upsurge and the proliferation of small arms have exacerbated the situation.

EPaRDA has been a pioneer in attempting to facilitate and organize in-country and cross-border (Kenya and Ethiopia) conflict mitigation and peace building initiatives for the pastoralist communities in the south Omo. It has conducted a series of peace making processes, of which the first one was made among Arore, Hamer and Borana. The second peacemaking was among Mursi, Ari, and Bodi and the third among Hamer,Dassenech , Nyangatom, Karo, Muguji, Mursi and Bodi. In addition, there were several other internal efforts and and cross-border peacemaking endeavors at reducing tensions between the Turkana of Kenya and Dassenech and Nyangatom of Kenya.

EPaRDA brought face to face various rival neighboring pastoral ethnic groups to discuss long standing rivalries. Using traditional peace settlement and conflict mitigation mechanisms this resulted in unprecedented peaceful coexistence among pastoralists that were historic enemies. The methodologies used by EPaRDA to implement conflict mitigation and peace building included; utilizing traditional peace settlement mechanisms by organizing local peace conference with the representatives from the government, conflicting groups and other actors; traditional elders managed all-inclusive inter community dialogues ; peace week celebrations; conflict mitigation and peace building training, and working with government at all levels to establish peace committees and enhance development that creates suitable conditions for sustainable peace. The inclusion of the women and the youth of the ethnic groups in the peace dialogues have been proved in making the peace building effective.
